## Two Languages, One Eye

I didn't pick up a camera because I wanted to be a photographer. I picked one up because I was tired of looking at screens and wanted to see the world differently.

What I didn't expect was how much the programmer's mind would influence the photographs — and how much the photographer's eye would sharpen the programmer.

## The Algorithm of a Good Frame

A photograph, like a function, has inputs and outputs. The inputs are light, subject, angle, timing, and depth of field. The output is the emotional response the image creates in a viewer.

The *algorithm* connecting them is composition.

Programmers are trained to think about efficiency — how do you achieve the desired output with the minimum necessary inputs? Photography asks the same question. What's *necessary* in this frame? What can you remove and still preserve meaning?

**Rule of thirds** is just a grid — a data structure that organizes where attention flows. **Leading lines** are directional vectors pulling the viewer's gaze toward the subject. **Negative space** is the equivalent of whitespace in code: it lets the important things breathe.

## Debugging a Bad Photo

When a photo doesn't work, I debug it like code.

*What was I trying to communicate?* (What should this function do?)

*What did I actually capture?* (What does it actually do?)

The gap between those two answers is the bug. Sometimes it's exposure — the technical layer. Sometimes it's composition — the logical layer. Sometimes it's timing — you had the right idea but missed the moment of peak expression.

The fix is the same as debugging: isolate the variable that's failing, and change only that.

## The Rajasthan Light

Sri Ganganagar, my hometown in Rajasthan, has a quality of light I've never seen replicated. The desert air filters dust into the atmosphere differently than anywhere else. Golden hour isn't golden — it's *amber*. Warm, heavy, almost liquid.

Photographing that light was what made me serious about the craft. When you find a subject that challenges your eye this much, you keep coming back.

## Patience as a Shared Virtue

Both programming and photography reward patience disproportionately.

In code: the problem you spend an hour staring at before writing anything is usually the one you solve fastest. The programmer who rushes to the keyboard first rarely wins.

In photography: the photographer who waits for the right moment — who holds position for thirty minutes for the light to change, for the subject to turn, for the crowd to thin — comes home with the shot.

Patience isn't passive. It's active waiting with clear intention.

## What I Shoot

Mostly whatever is in front of me when the light is interesting. That's the honest answer.

More specifically: architecture when I'm in cities — the geometry of buildings against sky. Landscapes in Rajasthan. Portraits occasionally, though I'm still learning to make people comfortable in front of a lens.

The photography section of this portfolio is where those frames live. They're not the work of a professional photographer. They're the work of a programmer who learned to see.
